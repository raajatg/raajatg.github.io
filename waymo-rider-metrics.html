ME RESUME

Measuring Rider Experiences

UXR Project at Waymo

April 2023 - May 2023

I designed a system that combines a set of quantitative driving metrics with an unintrusive rider feedback mechanism to find actionable opportunities to improve the end-to-end rider experience in Waymo vehicles.
Research Goal

What if Waymo riders’ end-to-end ride experiences could be perfectly understood, and turned into actionable recommendations for improvement?

Despite Waymo requesting post-trip feedback, most riders either don’t complete the form, or give a 5-star rating. Without good signal, Waymo can’t easily identify how to improve the driving experience.

Solution Benefits

Our new framework for capturing the rider experience accurately tracks riders’ overall experience from a qualitative and quantitative lens:

  1. It minimizes riders’ effort needed to give actionable feedback while improving feedback actionability

  2. It improves signal quality by capturing feedback in-context so riders don’t need to think back to their experience quality

  3. It provides Waymo’s Product Managers far clearer, constantly updated hillclimbing metrics to track progress against

UX Research Lead

Moving Past Star Ratings

I investigated the shortcomings of our existing post-trip feedback system (asking riders to provide a 5-star rating) and leverage literature reviews for inspiration on a better path forward.

Literature Review

How effective are traditional 5-star rating systems?

Our 5-star rating system had little to no correlation to rider usage rate, churn, or our internal ride quality metrics. But if they were ineffective, was there a better alternative?

A literature review of rating systems helped create some clarity:

1

Emotions over Stars: Emotions (with explicit labels) are far easier for people to relate to than abstract stars and improve consistency in ratings across users

2

A Single Rating is too Noisy: An overarching score doesn’t provide nuance on where to improve on a service offering; we need scores for specific areas

3

Find the “Acceptability Line”: Although a bipolar scale provides granularity, we need to understand what score is considered an acceptable experience across riders

Quantitative Data Analysis

How should we break down the rider experience?

Instead of a singular rating system, we needed to separate the ride experience into its salient components from a rider perspective, which we did by analyzing and clustering open-text app feedback.

We arrived at six buckets, which we dubbed the “rider experience outcomes”:

Availability: Can you easily hail a car when you need one?

Coverage: Does our service cover the places you need to go?

Pickups & Dropoffs (PUDOs): How well did we execute the pickup / dropoff?

Driving Experience: Is the driving smooth, pleasant and considerate of road rules?

Routing: How straightforward is the path the car takes from origin to destination?

Interruptions: How well do we respond to interruptions in the ride experience?

ISSUE:

We needed to identify what the major components were of Waymo riders’ experience, and shift away from a single overall rating

Understanding Rider Experience Influencers

I led 40+ in-field sessions and 80+ rides with Waymo riders to capture which factors mattered most for each of our six rider experience outcomes

Survey Design + Ride-Alongs

What factors most influence if a bucket is “acceptable”?

Even though we had our buckets, we didn’t know how to measure if a rider’s experience was acceptable or not for that bucket, or what factors were most influential in their decision.

To address these gaps, I developed a survey to administer to riders in-situ while going on Waymo rides with them. It helped address some major gaps:

QUESTION:

IDEA:

Do riders understand a bi-polar emotional scale better than star ratings?

Administer the scale in live time as riders go on Waymo rides, and ask them about their interpretation of the scale

What score on a scale corresponds to an acceptable service delivery?

Ask riders to provide an “acceptable / unacceptable” rating for each bucket and calculate the relationship to the scale

What factors are most influential in riders’ scoring decisions across buckets?

Start with hypotheses from open-ended feedback and add these as double-clicks for when riders are dissatisfied with a bucket; measure the frequency of each option’s selection

Contextual Inquiry + In-Depth Interviews

What is the “perfect experience” to benchmark against?

Although our survey was helpful in validating a bi-polar emotional scale and calibrating it to an acceptability threshold, it didn’t help us understand how to improve.

Instead, we accompanied the survey with in-depth conversations with riders that helped us accomplish two main goals:

1

Capture all influencing factors:s We talked to riders about the rationale for all of their scores, and uncovered factors that barely showed up in open-text feedback but were integral to building a good experience

2

Understand the “perfect experience”: We also deep-dove not just into what was a bad experience, but what would make an experience good – this helped shape benchmarks for teams to hillclimb against

We supplemented our survey with in-depth conversations with riders that taught us what the elements of a “perfect experience” were for each of our buckets

We learned...

We uncovered a wealth of information on what factors influenced which buckets, and the relative weighting of these factors. Beyond that:

Riders were consistent with how they interpreted the bi-polar emotional scale, and found it far less confusing than the star rating system

Waymo consistently delivered a worse experience across some buckets vs. others – PUDOs and routing surfaced as the two major issue areas

Riders’ definition of an “acceptable” score varied significantly between buckets and depended on that bucket’s impact on their safety (e.g., driving experience needed to be near-perfect to be acceptable)

Riders were far more receptive to providing feedback when in the car than once they were outside and busy with the rest of their lives

Translating Experiences to Metrics

I co-developed a rollup metric for each of the six rider experience outcomes, and developed a low-intensity, high-signal rider feedback system to replace post-trip feedback

Cross-Functional Collaboration + Brainstorming Sessions

How can we translate subjective rider feedback to product-friendly metrics?

Although our study findings were helpful, we needed a plan of action on how they could be embedded across product and engineering to drive meaningful change in our self-driving software capabilities.

Over the course of several collaboration and brainstorming session, I worked with the six product manager leads for each rider experience outcome to co-develop a single rollup metric that they could hillclimb against.

Single rollup metric: In response to PM feedback, we co-developed a single metric for each bucket (% of rides with an acceptable bucket experience) to streamline progress tracking

Sub-metrics with weightings: Each rollup metric was composed of weighted sub-metrics, with sub-metrics and weightings based on how many riders cited that factor as influencing overall bucket experience

Benchmark against rider feedback: We co-developed a “perfect” score for each metric based on what riders told us was a perfect experience for each factor, giving us an easy mark to hillclimb against

Survey Design

How can we continue to track rider feedback accurately and unintrusively?

Even with a set of metrics to hillclimb against, our PMs needed to continue to calibrate and check that metric signals were aligned with rider experiences.

I proposed a new solution for an in-car survey mechanism:

CONSTRAINT:

IDEA:

PMs needed constant feedback on rider sentiment for each bucket

Switch to soliciting trip feedback on a bi-polar emotional scale for each bucket (instead of overall)

Riders dislike providing feedback constantly, and at the end of trips

Ask for feedback on the in-car display, at most once every three rides to reduce rider survey fatigue

Feedback reliability drops as more time passes

Ask for feedback immediately after a relevant event occurs to reduce issues with poor recollection

We developed a brand-new method for collecting feedback on an ad-hoc basis tied directly to the context of rider experiences; less intrusive, more accurate, and constantly running

We learned...

We conducted initial testing feedback mechanism system by launching it to a subset of riders:

Riders’ completion percentage jumped from under 10% to over 80% by switching from post-trip to in-ride

Instead of collecting feedback once every X rides, riders felt it would be better to ask a question once every X in-car minutes, to prevent feedback after a few very short rides

Our initial weights for metrics were (mostly) accurate, and gave us further confidence in the efficacy of this system when scaled

Final Solution

The final rider experience measurement system (dubbed the Service Quality Framework) combines rider feedback inputs with constant measurements of driving quality to drive service improvements

Capture Contextual Rider Feedback

Capture rider feedback in the moment by asking about ride experience elements live (e.g., ask about how a pickup was immediately after a rider boards)

Use an emotion-based bi-polar scale with clear labeling so scores are easier for riders to interpret, and so provide less noisy signal



Our new system can ask about up to 11 topics through the in-car display, but limits itself to asking a question once every 60 ride minutes.

Deep-Dive into Areas of Improvement

Capture the factors that drive a poor experience whenever users provide a non-perfect score through an easy multiple choice selection, informed by past research

Allow riders to provide detailed feedback with an accompanying page on the app to provide detailed, open-ended feedback as needed

Illustrative view of the in-car display. Top: Question about pickup experience; Bottom: Opportunity for additional feedback if experience was subpar.

Track Changes through an Internal Dashboard

Identify the largest gaps in rider experience across all six rider experience outcomes to identify which buckets need more attention as Waymo’s driving capabilities evolve

Validate metric accuracy by identifying how often riders and our internal metrics both flagged a factor as being unacceptable, thereby improving metric accuracy

Our internal dashboard tracks our metric performance across buckets, and will compare it to rider assessments of each bucket to identify gaps.

Results & Final Thoughts

Our new metrics system launched in mid-2023 and our new rider feedback collection system is expected to launch shortly.

A better pulse on rider feedback

Waymo reorganized its product teams to be in line with the six rider experience outcomes defined in the new Service Quality Framework, with each team responsible for one of the metric we co-developed. These metrics are now being used to hillclimb against and improve the Waymo Driver.

Designs for the new rider feedback system are still being finalized, with the expectation of launching the new system shortly after.
