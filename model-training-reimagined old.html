Model Training Reimagined

MHCI Capstone Project with Bloomberg LP

February 2021 - Present

I redesigned Bloomberg’s machine learning model training process, so analysts without technical backgrounds can quickly and effectively train models that output to Bloomberg’s Terminal.

Research Goal

How might we improve the understandability of the model development lifecycle to empower Bloomberg's data analysts?

Bloomberg’s Terminal consolidates information across millions of financial documents, with ML models that identify and extract key datapoints.

Business analysts have the domain expertise needed to train the models but lack the technical skills needed to understand the process. Bridging this gap is critical so analysts can systemize their knowledge.

Solution Benefits (so far)

Compared to the current model development lifecycle used by analysts, our proposed process offers three core benefits:

  1. It reduces the number of steps in the model-training process by over 25% without tradeoffs in model performance

  2. It improves system understandability by 55% for analysts, so they feel confident moving quickly through the process

  3. It guides analysts through training so they can more easily identify model issues and train more accurate models

Remote Contextual Inquiries

How is the current model-training experience for analysts?

Bloomberg already had a model-training interface in place – “AutoModeler”. However, analysts struggled to use and understand AutoModeler to build effective models. AutoModeler’s product managers weren’t sure why.

Our team needed to uncover the lived experience of building projects in AutoModeler. We were dealing with two constraints:

1

Even with access to the tool, we couldn’t effectively simulate the range of model-building experiences analysts underwent

2

All analysts were remote due to COVID-19, so we couldn’t sit in on their daily model-building

To address these two constraints, I constructed a remote contextual inquiry method:

SHOW AND TELL INQUIRY:

Analysts share their screen as they run us through their most recently built model and relive their decisions and issues with AutoModeler

Participatory Design

How do users define understandability?

Although we knew that understandability was important to users across the model-building process, and we had learned some strategies for improving understandability, we weren’t sure if users wanted the same level of hand-holding and guidance across all stages.

How can design metaphors help?

1

Start by imagining different ways users could interact with the system in a future-state, along various levers

2

Combine complementary ways into a defined relationship illustrated by a clear metaphor

3

Users map their ideal “relationship” with the model at each stage using the easily grasped metaphors (as opposed to abstract concepts)

A completed design metaphors session

A completed design metaphors session
We learned...

Novices have a more effective learning experience when they can work through a problem independently, with constant feedback.

Novices enjoyed receiving machine over human support because it felt less judgmental and “nagging”.

Users wanted drastically different levels of guidance, feedback, and understandability at each phase of the process

Annotation and evaluation were especially confusing; analysts needed disproportionately more guidance and feedback in these stages

Aligning on an Ideal State

I co-developed 30+ ideas to improve the model-training process across all four stages, and performed a mix of quantitative and qualitative analysis to prioritize 8 features for implementation

Evidence-Based Design + Storyboarding + Speed Dating

How could we incorporate our findings to improve AutoModeler?

At the onset of the project, our team had hundreds of ideas to potentially improve AutoModeler. But with the research we had done, we finally had four guardrails on what good ideas looked like.

With these guiding inputs in mind, we developed 30 storyboards to represent our ideas across all four model-building phases. We asked users to rank ideas within each phase, and provide qualitative feedback.

A sample storyboard for our highest-performing idea

A sample storyboard for our highest-performing idea

Storyboarding Guiding Principles

They needed to bridge users’ technical gaps, help them find patterns, control others’ inputs, and incorporate preferences from previous projects (from Show and Tell)

They needed to address specific pain points in the model-building process across the stages (from User Journey Map)

They needed to encourage long-term learning by providing ample feedback for users to learn the next best step for themselves (from analogous domains)

They needed to provide more guidance and feedback during annotation and evaluation, and less during upload and training (from design metaphors)

Survey Analysis + Storyboard Synthesis Framework

How do we prioritize ideas to build out into features?

To turn 30 ideas into a more manageable feature set, we mixed quantitative and qualitative analyses of analysts’ feedback across 20 interviews:

Quantitative Analysis:

All our analysts had ranked ideas for each stage, and provided a score (out of 5) signifying their likeliness of using each idea.

We used this metrics to graph each stage’s idea, and identify the ideas that were meaningfully separated as likely to be used, and ranked highly.

ANALYSIS RESULTS:

We extracted 10 ideas across all four stages that were clear-cut favorites from analysts as improving understandability and empowering analyst decision-making

Qualitative Analysis:

We noted all qualitative feedback on each idea with a storyboard synthesis framework, separating moments of delight and dislike across ideas.

We examined ways that we could incorporate the highlights and minimize pain points across ideas to maximize the potential of the priority ideas.

ANALYSIS RESULTS:

From our analysis, we emerged with 8 distinct features that were built upon our prioritized ideas and that incorporated the most universally appreciated elements across our speed dating

Final Feature Propositions

Training machine learning models is a 4-stage process. I developed 8 recommendations across stages that makes the process more understandable to analysts, so they can identify the most effective tactics to maximize model performance with minimal effort:

Upload: Tie Data to Project Goals

During Upload, analysts receive overall project goals from their managers, then find and upload datasets that have examples of information they want their model to eventually extract. Two features can improve this stage:

  • Feature
  • Evidence
Document Library:

Uploaded documents can have metadata associated with them to help analysts easily sort, organize, and understand their datasets

Identified Need:

Analysts can stop uploading data when dataset distributions match what models would see in the real world, but there’s no way now to see what distributions look like

  • Feature
  • Evidence
Prior Project Base:

Analysts can import past datasets, annotations and model configurations during upload, as opposed to just being able to upload raw documentation

Identified Need:

Analysts often upload and label similar documents between projects, and can spend more time on duplicative work than value-additive training

Annotate: Understand Progress to Improve Efficiency

In Annotation, analysts manually label all elements in their uploaded documents that they eventually want their model to identify on its own (like creating an “answer key”). Two features in particular can speed up that process:

  • Feature
  • Evidence
Bird's Eye View:

Create summary visualizations of annotations to date so analysts can pinpoint gaps in their labeling work and prioritize certain documents or elements

Identified Need:

Today, analysts must rely on intuition and prior experience to know when labeling is done. This leads to premature finishes, and issues with model performance later

  • Feature
  • Evidence
Automatic Annotator:

Analysts annotate a subset of documents in detail. The system uses those labels as reference to create its own labeling rules and label the rest of the documents automatically

Identified Need:

Annotation is the most time-consuming, tedious part of model training, and is prone to errors and mistakes when done completely manually

Train: Optimize Input Selections

Once analysts label their documents, they then select machine learning functions and parameters that will be used to train the model so it can identify desired labels, even in unlabeled documents. Two features can guide analysts in making their selections:

  • Feature
  • Evidence
Model Training Optimizer:

Simultaneously train various model types and configurations to identify the best model for a specific project without analysts needing to select inputs.

Identified Need:

Analysts don’t know enough about machine learning to knowledgeably select the right models, but want to know that their selected inputs are the best possible ones

  • Feature
  • Evidence
Model Training Guide:

Integrate short, action-oriented explanations of ambiguous or highly technical terminology in the training process to guide users on what they can expect from adjusting inputs

Identified Need:

Analysts continually seek guidance from engineers on parameter inputs, which stresses out analysts and increases engineers’ workloads

Evaluate: Know Exactly How to Improve Performance

Once a model is trained, analysts must evaluate its performance to identify areas where it is performing poorly and determine how to drive improvement (often with additional annotations). Two features can help speed this process up:

  • Feature
  • Evidence
Error Explainer:

Identify patterns in where model predictions differ from labels, and point analysts towards documents that are especially problematic for re-annotation

Identified Need:

Today, analysts are shown a single table with every instance of where predictions differed from actual labels, with no prioritization or identification of problem areas

  • Feature
  • Evidence
Summary Reporting Tool:

Analysts can export quick summaries of model performance over time, highlight weaknesses, and share their perspectives on long-term achievable performance targets with managers

Identified Need:

Model goals are often determined independent of what analysts believe to be feasible, and lowering manager expectations can be difficult, especially because their time is limited.

Results & Final Thoughts

After aligning with Bloomberg's business and technical teams, I’ll be spending the summer semester building out the Features for annotation and evaluation.

Driving multi-stakeholder value

There was a fundamental flaw with our findings to date: they only considered analyst desirability, without thinking about the technical feasibility or commercial viability of these features.

We addressed this with the business prioritization matrix:

THE BUSINESS PRIORITIZATION MATRIX:

Ask AutoModeler’s engineers and product managers to map each feature’s impact to business and number of engineering resources required to gauge each feature's rough ROI

With our matrix, we’ve narrowed down our focus to 4 ideas within annotation and evaluation that we’ll be designing and prototyping for the remainder of the summer. With these 4 ideas, we aim to deliver significant value:

Analyst Benefits:

Reduce training time by 25% by significantly cutting down the number of steps in the process

Improve analysts’ understanding of the process and steps to take in ambiguous situations

Incorporate analysts’ knowledge into models better by making it easier to integrate their thought processes into model training

Bloomberg Benefits:

Improve information accuracy of Bloomberg Terminal with more accurate models powering outputs

Reduce engineering dependency, freeing them up to perform more value-additive tasks

Increase employee efficiency, allowing for more models to be trained to further automate Terminal’s information-gathering
