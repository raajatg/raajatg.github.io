GroGetters

November 2020 - December 2020

I designed a responsive mobile and watch application that helps Whole Food customers shop safely in-person at grocery stores during COVID-19. The app leverages AI to minimize users’ time in store, while ensuring they get every item on their list.

User Problem

COVID-19 has forced more people to buy groceries and cook at home. It has also put shoppers in an uncomfortable position – they still prefer to shop in-person but face increased anxiety from COVID-19 exposure risk and long, unwieldy grocery lists. How can we make in-person grocery shopping feel safe again?

Outcomes

In final testing, users agreed that GroGetters solved three of their critical needs at once:

  1. It minimized their cognitive load during a stressful experience by handling store navigation for them

  2. It helped them save money with deals that they had given up on trying to track

  3. It made them feel safer by minimizing both their time in-store and exposure risk to COVID-19.

Final Design

GroGetters is a mobile and watch application that tracks an optimal path through the grocery store based off customers’ grocery list to minimize their time in-store. It also assists users in crossing items off their list, with context-based deal recommendations and live location tracking to remind users to collect forgotten items.

Store Navigation

Charts the optimal path through the store by incorporating users’ list information, SKU data, and location data on the user and other app users. This minimizes users’ time in-store to reduce their risk of exposure to COVID-19.

Provides 3D navigation of a shelf once users near their item, offloading their cognitive load at a time when they are anxious and mistake-prone.

GroGetters: Watch (left) and Mobile (right) navigation

List Modification

Provides updating view of list that accounts for items already collected, helping users that often lose track of which items they already picked up.

Allows for list modification based off changing needs, accounting for “sudden recollection” of needs when users are in-store.

Offers touchless, voice-enabled list entries that minimizes contact with device surfaces to reduce spread of COVID-19.

GroGetters: Watch (left) and Mobile (right) list modification

Brand & Deal Discovery

Offers location-based and preference-based deal suggestions that identifies savings for users feeling too overwhelmed to organize their shopping around sales.

Easy addition of suggested items into user lists reduces cognitive load by only featuring deals when users near items.

GroGetters: Watch (left) and Mobile (right) deal discovery and selection

Forgotten Item Prevention

Reminds users to go back for an item with haptic feedback if they didn’t pause at its location. This soothes shoppers’ anxiety around forgetting items and having to go back in-store.

Allows for easy dismissal and item removal from list in one stroke, accommodating users that change their minds and decide to forego an item.

Project Takeaways

I became far better at designing for multiple platforms while weaving in delightful interactions. I also learned the importance of diverging in the design process, and developing solutions that balanced ambition with feasibility.

I Became Better At...

Leveraging design patterns for intuitive designs: I realized that novelty and creativity can sometimes be detrimental, when existing interactions are already understod and preferred by users

Designing for multiple platforms: Learning how to translate information-rich mobile screens to a constrained watch interface was challenging and illuminative.

Creating more delightful interactions: I developed a deep understanding of how to effectively design intuitive and delightful experiences through micro-interactions.

Designing for mobile and watch forced us to think about different methods of interaction and how to prioritize information for the smaller watch screen

If I Went Back in Time, I Would...

Assess multiple potential solutions: In retrospect, our team aligned too quickly on a single solution without exploring other possibilities

Spend more time diverging: Our team also quickly decided to minimize time in-store, without properly considering other problem areas

Expand upon our multi-modal interactions: I would have liked to have integrated multi-modal features more consistently across both devices, given initial positive feedback

Users responded favorably to voice searches on watch because they were hands-free – I wish we had integrated these multi-modal interactions more broadly

If I Had More Time, I Would...

Flesh out edge cases more: Our designs sometimes broke down with added complexity (e.g., how does the 3D map work when there are multiple items on the same shelf?)

Prototype within the context of use: We could learn a lot more with a higher-fidelity prototype that users could test in the context of shopping.

Assess GroGetter’s technical feasibility: I would have liked to move from design to development to test the feasibility of our optimization algorithm.

In-context testing would have helped us surface important issues

Diving into the Problem

How might we create an enjoyable and safe in-store grocery shopping experience in the midst of COVID-19?

How might we better understand users’ grocery shopping experiences?

To understand how to solve this complex problem, we first needed to better understand the in-store grocery shopping experience. To that end, our team interviewed:

13 American grocery shoppers of all ages, demographics, geographies, and SES. We didn’t want to narrow our target user without rationale.

We asked them to share their best and worst grocery shopping experience since the beginning of the pandemic. We used directed storytelling to immerse participants in their experience. We refrained from deeper contextual inquiries to be sensitive to social distancing requirements, and because we were under significant time pressure to capture initial insights.

To highlight the painpoints and moments of delight we heard from our interviews, we mapped them out on a user journey map and looked for areas of convergence between users.

We quickly realized that the in-store experience was the most painful part of the shopping experience. We also realized that this in-store experience was anchored by four key insights that both explained why shoppers went in-store, and why they hated it.

We learned...

Shoppers want the agency of shopping in-person and being able to inspect and pick their own perishables

Focusing on avoiding other shoppers meant shoppers made mistakes (e.g., forgetting things, getting the wrong item)

It takes too long to navigate the store and find items now that grocery lists are long and constantly changing

Shoppers are in too much of a rush to identify and capitalize on in-store sales and coupon opportunities

How do we identify a focus area within this space?

With research completed and critical insights extracted, we had a clear goal:

Help shoppers improve their in-store shopping experience by reducing their time in-store, reducing their cognitive load, and helping them save money


We wanted to ensure that we kept our user and their painpoints front and center as we shifted to the design process, so we developed a persona of our protypical user.

This captured all the major insights we had synthesized, but also kept the nuances of their motivations and goals for us to keep in mind during the design process (e.g. providing varied meals for their families)

Our final persona diagram laid out the major user goals, painpoints, and values that we needed to consider as we designed our final solution

Lo-Fi Designs

How might we design and test a compelling and immersive experience before even touching a computer?

How can we solve this complex problem?

Our team decided to sketch out quick ideas individually before coming back together and discussing the best path forward. As I sketched, I looked to our persona and a few key considerations:

1

How can we minimize shoppers’ time in the store?

2

How can we simplify all the information users are processing when navigating a store?

3

How can we uncover opportunities for shoppers to save money?

I thought about a pain point that users kept returning to when discussing their time in-store: navigation. With long lists and reduced cognitive loads, people didn’t know where to go for items, creating frustration and confusion.

I developed an idea for a navigational system that optimized shoppers’ path through the store based off their grocery list. It also provided information on relevant sales based on shoppers’ lists.

After discussion, we decided as a team to move forward with my idea and explore how it could address our user painpoints and needs.

IDEA:

What if shoppers were assisted by a navigational system that optimized their path through the store based off their grocery list and opportunities for savings?

How can we turn an idea into a testable prototype?

I fleshed out my initial sketches into a paper prototype. I focused on developing 3 core features:

Solution Features:

1

Path Optimizer: Navigates users through grocery stores as quickly as possible based off the grocery list they have

2

List Modifier: Give users the flexibility to view and modify their shopping list at any point in store, with their navigation system updating automatically

3

Personalized Item Recommendations: Promote deals and recommended brands for users based off the items on their list

I realized that this app’s value would be far more pronounced within the context of use, but that it’s hard to ask users to imagine that context for a rough paper mockup.

To create that context, I combined these screens with a set of brief storyboarding panels to immerse users in familiar situations and help them think about the issues they’d have with the screens during those situations. I coined this hybrid storyboarding-prototyping model “story-typing”.

We asked users to walk through our “story-types” through a think-aloud protocol, and uncovered some critical insights.

We created a hybrid of a storyboard and paper prototype to help our testers understand both the context of use and demo the design itself

We learned...

Show users what’s relevant: There was too much information on the screens that users had to juggle between and remember how to access.

Users want clarity up-front: Users liked having complete control over the lists they selected and how they edited them before going in-store.

Don’t make users use their hands: A subset of users wanted to access the app’s information without using their hands at all, to free themselves to shop for items

Mid-Fi Designs

How might we translate initial user feedback into a cohesive and impactful mobile and watch design?

How can we help shoppers easily get the information they need?

1. How do we make information more accessible?

Shoppers wanted to always see navigational instructions and know where to go next, even as they accessed other information like lists or deals.

I opted for a modal view that users could access from the navigation page. Everything was now on one page, removing the need to toggle between pages. Even after a modal was opened, navigation could be viewed in the background.

2. How do we prevent information overload?

We also didn’t want to inundate users with deals and savings on items that were further down the list before users could leverage that information.

To keep things relevant, I used geo-tracking to see which item users were closest to. When users selected the deals modal, they would only see deals and suggestions for that closest item, to inform immediate decision-making.

If a user was near another item and wanted to plan ahead, they could click that item’s icon on the map to pull up its relevant deals

We also saved selected preferences so the information could become more and more relevant with time as we learned user preferences.

ISSUE:

Any information that isn’t immediately relevant just adds to cognitive load



SOLUTION:

Only present deals for the geographically closest item, to inform item selection



How can we ease shoppers’ anxiety before they enter the grocery store?

During a shopping experience, users’ hurry and anxiety makes them want the exact information needed to inform the next decision. Any information that isn’t immediately relevant just adds to the stress.

Before a shopping experience, users want as much information as possible to soothe anxiety and help them feel in control. I used this distinction to redesign the pre-shopping flow.

Now users were guided through the specific store and list they wanted to select for their shopping flow. Instead of trying to guess the list users wanted to use, this flow showed them their options, and allowed them to edit lists before entering the store so they felt in control once navigation began.

ISSUE:

Shoppers don't feel in control of their lists when they enter a store



SOLUTION:

Review users' store location and list items up-front to instill confidence



How can users access relevant information… without using their hands?

Operating a mobile phone tends to mean using your hands. But for a distinct subset of users, keeping their hands occupied with their phone meant their hands weren’t free to grab items off shelves, and meant they were touching their phone too much for comfort (with the potential of COVID spread on surfaces).

Our team thought of a few ways to address this issue and conducted some rapid guerilla research to understand sentiment towards our ideas:

IDEA:

FEEDBACK:

What if we used voice-based interactions?

The information is too complex to be easily digested via voice

What if we designed a strap to affix the phone to shopping carts?

It’s uncomfortable to place my phone against a cart that thousands have touched

What if we coupled the phone with a watch interface?

Just make sure to keep the mobile interface for people that don’t have smart watches!



The people had spoken. We kept our mobile designs, but worked to translate them into an accompanying watch interface that would push relevant navigation and deals as notifications so users didn’t need their hands.

I collaborated to ensure the most salient elements of the mobile design were translated well into the watch experience. Because of the watch’s limited real estate, this meant being disciplined with showing only the most critical information for decision-making, which we broke up into distinct screens.

ISSUE:

Shoppers don't feel comfortable using their hands to access information for convenience and hygiene reasons



SOLUTION:

Complement the mobile app with a watch-based interface to push information while keeping hands free



We learned...

We tested our refreshed mobile designs by asking users to walk around their home and use the prototype on their phone as if they were walking through a grocery store, making turns in their home based off app navigations and plucking items from the air as if they were at a shelf.

This immersive experience gave us a new set of takeaways.

It’s too taxing to always monitor the app for prompts on what to do next, but there’s no support for users that miss a prompt.

Navigating to the right aisle is only half the battle; shoppers also struggled to find the right item on crowded shelves.

Navigating the app required far too much precision, and users frequently erred in their selections

Hi-Fi Designs

How might we translate user feedback into a complementary mobile and watch flow that's developer-ready?

How can we reliably deliver information to users without being annoying?

Our users had made it clear that they didn’t want to be inundated with notifications and prompts for every element of their shopping experience because of how overwhelming it was.

At the same time, because the current design relied on users to keep a close eye on the on-screen information without any notifications to alert them to major instructions, users would overlook instructions and walk past items on their list. How could we strike a balance?

I realized there were a few questions I needed to think through to find the right solution:

QUESTION:

IDEA:

When would users be ok with receiving notifications?

Shoppers wanted a reminder about forgotten items before they left an aisle to prevent backtracking (from mid-fi think-alouds)

How could we determine when a user had forgotten an item?

Geo-tracking could identify if shoppers paused to pick up an item or sped past it, at which point they’d need reminding

What kind of notification would be most effective at assisting users?

Throw subtlety out the window; the priority is minimal back-tracking. Full-screen notifications with haptic feedback are reliable design patterns to notify users.



My final solution was a “fail-safe” mechanism that reminded users to pick up an item if they sped past it. We also adapted this solution to the watch interface, striking a balance between being helpful and remaining unintrusive unless needed.

ISSUE:

Shoppers want to ensure they don't forget items, but don't want constant notifications while they shop



SOLUTION:

Push notifications only when users seem to be moving past an item without pausing at it, based off geo-tracking



How can we provide navigational assistance at different levels of granularity?

So you’ve gotten to the right spot in the aisle… now what? Many of our users noted that they’d spend minutes just trying to find an item on the shelf because shelf layouts were so unintuitive.

The main issue seemed to be knowing what row an item was on. I decided to help by providing a shelf-level navigation feature, which created a 3D view of the shelves and helped shoppers identify exactly where their item was.

A 3D view in particular helped users that didn’t want to think about shelf numbers or grids. Many of them had responded well to the visual layout of our navigation system to date, and remaining visual-first in our navigation was a focus that we tried to maintain.

I don’t know about you, but I think I spend more time trying to find the exact item I need on a shelf than I do actually getting to the right aisle. Aisle labels are large and clear. Shelf labels are basically non-existent
How can we reduce the amount of precision required to navigate our app?

We had used tap-based interactions throughout our app to add and remove items from lists, and to highlight brand preferences. These were too susceptible to “fat-finger error” and needed too much precision.

To replace them, I turned to established design patterns. I learned about the movement towards swipe-based interactions and some of their major benefits:

1

They required little precision in movement (swipes are more forgiving than taps)

2

They provided feedforward on actions (beginning a swipe indicates what will happen)

3

They turn two-gesture interactions or screens cluttered with buttons into simpler, cleaner displays that can focus on maximizing relevant information

I made the switch to swipe-based interactions, and saw an immediate improvement in usability.

ISSUE:

Shoppers that are moving quickly through a grocery store struggle with tap-based interactions and the precision required



SOLUTION:

Replace tap-based navigation with forgiving swipe-based interactions



Swipe-based interactions on the item list view, helping users add and remove items easily

Results & Final Thoughts

I helped design a solution that eased shoppers' anxieties around shopping in-person, and made them feel confident getting their groceries during COVID-19.

Making COVID-19 a little less scary

I'm proud of how I learned to design for multiple platforms. I realized that designing for multiple platforms isn’t about replicating an experience – it’s about delivering the same value proposition while accounting for each interface’s context of use and capabilities.

I'm also proud of our final design for being able to drive value for both shoppers and businesses alike:

Shopper Benefits:

Reduced time in store: Reduced chance of exposure to COVID-19

Increased satisfaction: Frictionless experiences through stores that rival the best online grocery shopping processes

Reduced average price-per-item-paid Capitalize on deals to save money and reduce costs on a per-item basis

Business Benefits:

Increased in-store spending: More customers willing to shop in-store

Increased cross-sell rates: Opportunity for stores to push recommendations for nearby attractive grocery items

Increased conversion on in-store deals: Create more intelligent promotion campaigns based off uptake on deals
