RAAJAT GUPTA
ME RESUME

GroGetters

UX Research, Usability Testing, Ethnographic Research

November 2020 - December 2020

I designed a responsive mobile and watch application that helps Whole Food customers shop safely in-person at grocery stores during COVID-19. The app leverages AI to minimize users’ time in store, while ensuring they get every item on their list.

User Problem

How can we make in-person grocery shopping feel safe again?

COVID-19 has forced more people to buy groceries and cook at home. It has also put shoppers in an uncomfortable position – they still prefer to shop in-person but face increased anxiety from COVID-19 exposure risk and long, unwieldy grocery lists.

Outcomes

  1. GroGetters minimizes shoppers’ cognitive load during a stressful experience by handling store navigation for them

  2. It helps shoppers save money with deals that they had given up on trying to track

  3. It makes shoppers feel safer by minimizing both their time in-store and exposure risk to COVID-19.

My Role

UX Researcher + Designer

I conducted user interviews and uncovered the major painpoints in the grocery shopping experience.

I designed the mobile prototype from lo-fi to hi-fi, coordinating efforts with the smart watch designers to ensure consistency.

Final Design

GroGetters is a mobile and watch application that charts the optimal path through Whole Foods stores based off shoppers’ grocery lists. It tracks item retrieval, recommends relevant deals, and identifies missed items for shoppers’ ease of mind.

Reduces shoppers' time in store

Charts the optimal path through the store by incorporating users’ list information, SKU data, and location data on the user and other app users

Offers 3D shelf navigation once users near their item, reducing the time spent stationary within aisles.

Shoppers don’t need to think about where to go next as the app tracks the optimal store path for them

Updates and tracks shopping lists

Updates grocery lists as users pass by items in-store, helping users keep track of which items they already picked up.

Offers text-based and voice-based list additions for users that want to minimize contact with device surfaces and reduce spread of COVID-19.

Shoppers can track the items they’ve collected, add / remove items, and do it all without touching their device

Helps shoppers save money

Suggests location-based and preference-based deal recommendations for users that can’t identify relevant sales before visiting stores

Allows addition of suggested items onto lists to understand user preferences and promote increasingly relevant deals over time

Shoppers are suggested deals only once they near relevant items, helping reduce their cognitive load

Forgotten Item Prevention

Reminds shoppers to go back for an item with haptic feedback if they didn’t pause at its location, providing a safety net for rushed shoppers

Allows for easy item removal from list, for users that change their minds and decide to forego a missed item.

Shoppers don’t have to worry about missing items, as GroGetters provides them a safety net when they forget something

Diving into the Problem

I interviewed 13 American shoppers to understand what parts of the in-store shopping experience were the most painful, and why.

Directed Storytelling + Customer Journey Maps

How might we better understand users’ grocery shopping experiences?

I made a few key decisions as we tried to wrap our heads around the current in-person shopping journey:

1

Interview 13 diverse American grocery shoppers, of all ages, demographics, geographies and SES. This helped us get a broad view of the shopping experience

2

Use a directed storytelling method of their best and worst experiences. A more intimate contextual inquiry would involve shadowing, and would ignore social distancing requirements

Our interviews helped us understand the end-to-end grocery shopping experience. I mapped them on a user journey map to isolate the highs and lows of the process. The in-store experience quickly emerged as the most painful step.

We learned...

Shoppers want the agency of shopping in-person and being able to inspect and pick their own perishables

Focusing on avoiding other shoppers meant shoppers made mistakes (e.g., forgetting things, getting the wrong item)

It takes too long to navigate the store and find items now that grocery lists are long and constantly changing

Shoppers are in too much of a rush to identify and capitalize on in-store sales and coupon opportunities

Personas

How do we identify a focus area within this space?

I wanted to keep our user and their four salient pain points front and center as we shifted to the design process, so we developed a persona of our protypical user.

I made sure to retain the nuances of their motivations and goals to integrate during the design process (e.g. providing varied meals for their families).

The persona helped me realize the focal point of users’ pain: navigation. If users knew where to go to find their items at the best price, they would…

  • Make fewer mistakes forgetting or misidentifying items
  • Take less time in store trying to find items
  • Feel less anxious about missing sales / deals

I identified an opportunity space that our team elected to move forward with:



What if shoppers were assisted by a navigational system that optimized their path through the store based off their grocery list and opportunities for savings?

Our user persona helped us identify the single greatest need that connected all the discrete shopper pain points: navigation

Our final persona diagram laid out the major user goals, painpoints, and values that we needed to consider as we designed our final solution

Defining the Idea

I found a way to test our idea while helping users immerse themselves in the usage context through a novel prototyping method I invented – “StoryTyping”

"StoryTyping" (Storyboarding + Prototyping)

How can we turn an idea into a testable prototype?

We created a hybrid of a storyboard and paper prototype to help our testers understand both the context of use and demo the design itself

I fleshed out my initial sketches into a paper prototype. I focused on developing 3 core features:

Solution Features:

1

Path Optimizer: Navigates users through grocery stores as quickly as possible based off the grocery list they have

2

List Modifier: Give users the flexibility to view and modify their shopping list at any point in store, with their navigation system updating automatically

3

Personalized Item Recommendations: Promote deals and recommended brands for users based off the items on their list

This app’s value would be far more pronounced within the context of use, but that’s hard to imagine for a rough paper mockup. Something more elaborate was impossible with COVID-19 restrictions.

To work within our constraints, I developed a new prototyping methodology:



"STORYTYPING":

I combined our screens with a set of brief storyboarding panels to immerse users in familiar situations and think about the screens’ utility in those situations.

We created a hybrid of a storyboard and paper prototype to help our testers understand both the context of use and demo the design itself

Think-Aloud

We learned...

Show users what’s relevant: There was too much information on the screens that users had to juggle between and remember how to access.

Users want clarity up-front: Users liked having complete control over the lists they selected and how they edited them before going in-store.

Don’t make users use their hands: A subset of users wanted to access the app’s information without using their hands at all, to free themselves to shop for items

Developing Mobile Screens

I used initial prototype feedback to inform the development of a basic mobile and smartwatch prototype that we tested with an immersive think-aloud protocol.

Insight-Based Refinement + Interface Design

How can we help shoppers easily get the information they need?

There was a clear disconnect between the information users were getting and what they needed, as they complained about irrelevant and unclear communication. I identified three priority areas that we needed to address, along with potential solutions:

QUESTION:

IDEA:

How do we make it easier to access the information users need without hiding navigation guidance?

Put everything in one place, on one navigation screen with modal screens that could be opened without covering navigation guidance

How do we show users the information they need only when they need it?

Use geo-tracking to only show deal recommendations for items that users are close to on the deals modal

How can we help users feel in control before they step foot into the store?

Confirm the exact store and list users will be using before entering the store, with a quick “onboarding” flow

Insight-Based Refinement + Interface Design

How can users access relevant information… without using their hands?

For a distinct subset of users, keeping their hands occupied with their phone meant their hands weren’t free to grab items off shelves. With COVID-19 potentially spreading through surfaces, they were also uncomfortable repeatedly touching their phones.

Our team thought of a few ways to address this issue and conducted some rapid guerilla research to understand user sentiment:

IDEA:

FEEDBACK:

What if we used voice-based interactions?

The information is too complex to be easily digested via voice

What if we designed a strap to affix the phone to shopping carts?

It’s uncomfortable to place my phone against a cart that thousands have touched

What if we coupled the phone with a watch interface?

Perfect! Just make sure to also keep the mobile interface for people that don’t have smart watches.



Based off user feedback, I worked with the team’s smart watch designer to translate the mobile experience. This meant being disciplined with showing only the most critical information for decision-making, which we broke up into distinct screens.

Usability Testing + Think-Aloud

We learned...

We tested our refreshed mobile designs by asking users to walk around their home and use the prototype on their phone as if they were walking through a grocery store, making turns in their home based off app navigations and plucking items from the air as if they were at a shelf.

This immersive experience gave us a new set of takeaways.

It’s too taxing to always monitor the app for prompts on what to do next, but there’s no support for users that miss a prompt.

Navigating to the right aisle is only half the battle; shoppers also struggled to find the right item on crowded shelves.

Finalizing Designs

I incorporated user critiques to create a polished mobile flow that finally reduced the stressors of in-person grocery shopping.

Insight-Based Refinement + Interaction Design

How can we reliably deliver information to users without being annoying?

Our users didn’t want to be inundated with notifications and prompts for every element of their shopping experience. But without notifications and prompts, they would overlook instructions and walk past items on their list. How could we strike a balance?

I realized there were a few questions I needed to think through to find the right solution:

QUESTION:

IDEA:

When would users be ok with receiving notifications?

Shoppers wanted a reminder about forgotten items before they left an aisle to prevent backtracking (from mid-fi think-alouds)

How could we determine when a user had forgotten an item?

Geo-tracking could identify if shoppers paused to pick up an item or sped past it, at which point they’d need reminding

What kind of notification would be most effective at assisting users?

Throw subtlety out the window; the priority is minimal back-tracking. Full-screen notifications with haptic feedback are reliable design patterns to notify users.

ISSUE:

Shoppers want to ensure they don't forget items, but don't want constant notifications while they shop



SOLUTION:

Push notifications only when users seem to be moving past an item without pausing at it, based off geo-tracking

Insight-Based Refinement + Interaction Design

How can we provide navigational assistance at different levels of granularity?

Many of our users noted that they’d spend minutes just trying to find an item on the shelf because shelf layouts were so unintuitive. The main issue seemed to be knowing what row an item was on.

I had an idea for a shelf-level navigation feature, which created a 3D view of the shelves and helped shoppers identify exactly where their item was.

A 3D view in particular helped users that didn’t want to think about shelf numbers or grids. Many of them had responded well to the visual layout of our navigation system to date, and remaining visual-first in our navigation was a focus that we tried to maintain.

I don’t know about you, but I think I spend more time trying to find the exact item I need on a shelf than I do actually getting to the right aisle. Aisle labels are large and clear. Shelf labels are basically non-existent

Results & Final Thoughts

I helped design a solution that eased shoppers' anxieties around shopping in-person, and made them feel confident getting their groceries during COVID-19.

Making COVID-19 a little less scary

I'm proud of how I learned to design for multiple platforms. I realized that designing for multiple platforms isn’t about replicating an experience – it’s about delivering the same value proposition while accounting for each interface’s context of use and capabilities.

I'm also proud of our final design for being able to drive value for both shoppers and businesses alike:

Shopper Benefits:

Reduced time in store: Reduced chance of exposure to COVID-19

Increased satisfaction: Frictionless experiences through stores that rival the best online grocery shopping processes

Reduced average price-per-item-paid Capitalize on deals to save money and reduce costs on a per-item basis

Business Benefits:

Increased in-store spending: More customers willing to shop in-store

Increased cross-sell rates: Opportunity for stores to push recommendations for nearby attractive grocery items

Increased conversion on in-store deals: Create more intelligent promotion campaigns based off uptake on deals
