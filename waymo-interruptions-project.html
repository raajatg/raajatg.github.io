RAAJAT GUPTA
ME RESUME

Redesigning Ride Interruptions

UXR Project at Waymo

September 2022 - February 2023

I redesigned Waymo’s ride interruption flow, so riders experiencing temporary driving issues could quickly and effectively navigate next steps for staying safe and getting to their destination.

Research Goal

How might we ensure riders feel safe and able to progress to their destination if their self-driving vehicle experiences a driving interruption?

In 2022, Waymo was gearing up to significantly expand its miles driven and territories covered. Scaling would also mean an increase in strandings, when the car stops moving because the self-driving software can’t identify a path forward. We needed to redesign our in-car flow to help riders effectively navigate next steps across stranding types, and de-risk territory expansion.

Solution Benefits

Our final redesigned interruptions flow (now deployed across vehicles) meaningfully improved rider safety and satisfaction in three main ways:

  1. It integrates rider preferences into the rescue flow by allowing riders to decide next steps that work best for their needs in the moment

  2. It reduces erosion of rider trust after a stranding happens by quickly and safely getting them to their final destination

  3. It minimizes the human labor needed to rescue riders by removing dependencies on Rider Support and Waymo’s Roadside Assistance

Understand Interruptions Today

I conducted an audit of how effective our existing flow was by studying effective techniques for stress minimization, and interviewing Waymo employees who had experienced ride interruptions.

In-Depth Interviews + Experience Rewind

How effective is our current interruptions flow?

Before interviewing external riders, I wanted to quickly identify major areas of improvement from our interruptions flow to inform our redesign.

At this stage I quickly realized it was better to interview Waymo employees who had experienced strandings for two main reasons:

1

Employees can give us better feedback than public riders on how we handle busier areas because they test-ride in territories not yet accessible to public riders

2

Employees can be recruited in days for a quick turnaround, compared to weeks for recruiting public riders

Literature Review

What are generally effective techniques for reducing stress?

Interviews had given us a good idea of where gaps in Waymo’s current experience were, but to iterate on our interruptions flow, I wanted to draw inspiration from analogous sources.

I looked at how other organizations have effectively reduced stress for individuals, ranging from building evacuations to plane crashes.

Although a Waymo stranding in-road may be less stressful, these techniques resonated with the gaps we had identified from our own employees.

An active, problem-focused coping method led to higher satisfaction, enjoyment and desire to continue in stressful situations for athletes

We learned...

Riders need an explanation of why we’ve stranded and what’s happening next to feel like Waymo is prepared for this situation

Riders appreciate providing input on their preferences as Waymo determines next steps; this helps riders feel in control

As a stranding becomes higher-risk (e.g., middle of city vs. residential street), riders want Waymo to be more prescriptive on next step instead of soliciting input

A “human touch” may help reduce stress even further by building an emotional connection that fosters rider trust

Translate Rider Needs into a New Flow

In defining the new interruptions flow, I identified distinct flows needed for different stranding types, then led co-design sessions with Product to build flows that incorporated research findings.

How many distinct flows do we need to build and test?

One of the biggest takeaways from our interviews was how drastically riders’ preferences changes as perceived risk to their well-being changed. Roughly, there were three categories of strandings:

High-risk (e.g., railroad crossings): Imminent risk to life meant riders wanted to get out immediately and have a traditional human driver pick them up

Medium-risk (e.g., dense urban areas): High-visibility but lower danger meant riders were open to different options, but wanted a fast resolution with minimal waiting

Low-risk (e.g., residential street): When there’s nobody around, riders were comfortable with the existing flow (wait in the car for a human to drive the car manually)

How do we ensure our new flows address rider needs?

Test Dense Urban Stranding Flow

I parallel prototyped two flows with riders after simulating an actual dense urban stranding to immerse them in the stressors and time pressure involved when actually going through an interruption flow.

Parallel Prototyping + Usability Testing

How much input do riders want when they’re being rescued?

One of the team’s biggest open questions revolved around how much input riders wanted to give on their preferred next step after a stranding.

To answer, I proposed parallel prototyping to identify when riders wanted more or less prescriptiveness so we could then build a single flow. Our two flows were:

1

Tell the rider what’s happening: Provide a single path to resolution with updates on progress towards that resolution

2

Ask the rider what they want: Give riders options on resolution, and let them pick while supported by information we provide

ISSUE:

How can we determine if riders prefer a more prescriptive or soliciting approach to stranding rescues?



IDEA:

Parallel prototype two flows with drastically different prescriptiveness to identify what elements resonate from each

In-Field Immersion + Accessibility Evaluation

How do we safely immerse riders in the context of a dense urban stranding?

It’s impossible to properly understand what flow riders would prefer in real life without introducing the stress / time pressure of the situation, so I developed an immersive testing solution:

CONSTRAINT:

SOLUTION:

Riders should feel stress and time pressure throughout the process

Unexpectedly strand the car in downtown Tempe; hire an actor to honk the car and swerve past during the flow

Riders should never be in any actual danger of a collision

Strand the car on a side street right off the main road; get the ambiance without the risk

Low-vision riders can’t interact with paper prototypes and aren’t based in Phoenix

Conduct an audio-based remote test with sound effects of cars driving and honking, with the flow read aloud

We drove riders through Phoenix then intentionally triggered a stranding on a busy side street; we then talked through paper prototypes with riders

We learned...

Our novel prototyping method immersed users and helped us capture reliable feedback, such as:

Our riders prefer giving input on next steps because it allows them to integrate their ride-specific contextual needs (e.g., minimal effort, fastest ETA) into every situation

Soliciting next steps is only effective if we provide all the information riders need to make a decision (e.g., steps in process, ETAs) and allow easy comparison

Once riders decide what rescue flow they want to pursue, they no longer want optionality; instead, clearly lay out next steps and our progress in live time

If we can present information quickly and accurately, then riders no longer want any humans on the line to support – that just adds stress and artificial time pressure to make a decision

Test Railroad Tracks Stranding Flow

I immersed riders in a fake stranding near tracks and tested two alternative rescue flows with them to determine if riders would prefer evacuating or getting driven off tracks in this stranding context.

Usability Testing + Accessibility Evaluation

How do we know if riders can be trusted to evacuate in time?

Our design team had created two alternative flows that relied on different assumptions about riders being true:

FLOW:

ASSUMPTION:

Tell riders to evacuate ASAP

Riders would be able to evacuate a Waymo vehicle in under 15 seconds if needed

Tell riders to stay put as we drive them off tracks shortly

Riders can’t evacuate quickly and it’s faster to restart our software and drive off the tracks



To test which assumption was true, we tested each flow separately with both able-bodied and low-vision / low-hearing riders, one after the other in a random order.

This helped us understand the spectrum of reaction times and rider willingness to both stay in the vehicle or evacuate suddenly, which ultimately shaped our final recommendations.

Participant Priming + In-Field Immersion

How can we safely simulate the urgency of stranding on train tracks?

But findings from these tests would be unreliable unless we could (safely) simulate the level of urgency riders would feel when on train tracks.

We integrated three components as part of a fully immersive ride-along with riders that included a fake stranding along their route:

1

Stranding near train tracks: During our fake strandings, we stopped the car ~50 feet from train tracks to increase the feeling of danger without compromising rider safety

2

Digital stress test: Before fake stranding, we administered an online questionnaire that increased rider stress safely (e.g., through timed math questions)

3

Immersive audio experience: Once we began going through the flows, we played ambient sounds (e.g., train horns, gate bells) as riders went through the flow

To immerse riders in the context of a railroad stranding, we pretended to strand close to actual railroad tracks and timed our stranding with the train schedule

To immerse riders in the context of a railroad stranding, we pretended to strand close to actual railroad tracks and timed our stranding with the train schedule
We learned...

Most riders exited the vehicle in under 15 seconds, but some couldn’t or spent too long corralling their belongings. It was unsafe to rely on riders to rescue themselves

Riders complied when asked to stay inside the vehicle, but only if they were updated constantly on the vehicle status to reassure them we were not stalled

Once they are in a safe position, riders need a human to check in on their well-being and reassure them, or their trust in Waymo is irreparably shattered

Final Solution

We finalized two entirely new interruption flows – one for dense urban strandings where riders could collaborate on next steps, and one on railroad tracks where we got riders to safety ourselves ASAP.

Solicit Rider Input on Next Steps

Provide riders with options on how they can continue their ride, and give them the information they need to make an informed decision (e.g., ETA, process steps)

Provide live-time progress updates once a rider selects an option to maintain trust in our rescue process and encourage future usage of Waymo



Top: Riders are provided on options for next steps once stranded; Bottom: After selecting, riders see step-by-step progress updates

Accelerate Riders off Railroad Tracks

Give riders the option to exit early when no train is detected to minimize the chances they’re subjected to the stressors of being on tracks with a train coming

Ask riders to stay in the car once a train is coming because riders may not have time to exit on their own quickly enough, and will obey our instructions if we act fast

Top: Riders are given the option to leave initially; Bottom: Once a train is detected, riders are asked to stay inside

Reduce the Role of Rider Support

Replace Rider Support entirely for dense urban strandings because riders prefer having time, privacy, and information at their pace to decide how they want to proceed

Keep Rider Support for high-stress situation like checking in on rider well-being after a railroad tracks stranding, or as an option if our in-car display is confusing

Rider Support is no longer looped in by default unless riders are under extreme stress

Results & Final Thoughts

Through our rounds of usability testing, we finalized and launched two entirely new flows to support riders across a host of stranding situations.

Maintaining rider trust during strandings

Our dense urban strandings flow launched in summer 2023 and immediately helped riders feel heard and prioritized instead of being shoehorned into next steps that weren’t aligned with their needs.

The findings from our railroad stranding testing allowed us to launch a finalized rescue flow in Q1 2023, and gave Waymo leadership confidence to increase our driving territory to cross railroad tracks.

It's scary to be stuck in the road with nobody to help... but this new process makes me feel like Waymo has my back and is doing everything to get me going again. It makes me feel like Waymo really cares about my safety.
Waymo Rider

Improve riders’ post-interruption impression of Waymo because of our improved handling of the situation

Waymo Benefits:
